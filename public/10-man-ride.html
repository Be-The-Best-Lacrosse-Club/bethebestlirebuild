The Threat

What is the 10-Man Ride?

Pioneered by Lars Tiffany at Virginia, the 10-man ride pulls the goalie out of the cage to cover a man. This frees up a defender to double-team the ball carrier at the midline — suffocating pressure, but the cage is empty. It's a high-risk, high-reward bet that we're going to beat.

Why the 10-Man is Falling Apart
  • National clear rates went from 83% (2019) to 86% (2023) — teams have the answer
  • Only 1 D1 team held opponents under 78% in 2023 (down from 10 teams in 2019)
  • Virginia's own ride effectiveness dropped — opponents now clear 85% against them
  • Full-field shots are now a scheme, not a prayer — Duke, ND, Cornell all install them
Step One

Recognize It Fast

You have 2 seconds to identify a 10-man ride. If we don't recognize it, we can't counter it. Every player on the field must know the triggers.

!

The Three Triggers

Call It Out Loud

When you see ANY of these, someone on the clearing team must yell "CAGE!" or "10-MAN!" — it's our trigger word.

  1. Goalie crosses the midline — or starts sprinting toward it. That's the #1 tell.
  2. All three of their attackmen are behind the midline pressuring — not just two.
  3. Double-team appears immediately at the midline — means their middle D-man is gambling.
Cue

The second you hear "CAGE!" — everyone runs their 10-man counter assignment. No thinking. Automatic response.

Recognition first, execution second. We've installed 5 counters. Pick the right one based on what the ride gives us.

Counter 01

Shoot the Dang Ball

The simplest counter — and the most demoralizing. When they empty the cage, our poles shoot from their own zone. Duke's Quin McMahon hits these with staff permission. Ours will too.

1

Full-Field Empty Net Shot

The Easiest Goal in the Game

Our defensemen and close-range middies are shooters now. If their goalie is 50+ yards out of position, you have staff permission to shoot.

Why It Works
  • Limited downside — a missed shot goes out of bounds or gets backed up. That's a free clear either way.
  • When it goes in, momentum flips violently — they stop running the ride for the rest of the game
  • Forces them to commit to a regular ride next possession — we've neutralized their scheme with one shot
D1 Shoots 70+ Yards · Empty Cage · Goalie Can't Recover
Coaching Cue

We will practice the 70-yard shot after every practice. Poles and d-middies only. Top ten shooters earn the green light in games.

Tell for the shot: Goalie is 40+ yards from cage AND moving away. If he's moving back toward the cage, hold the shot and execute Counter 2 instead.

Counter 02

Two-Diagonal Clear

The money clear. The go-to. Two quick diagonal passes and we're at the midline with a 6v5 going to goal. This is the play that beat Virginia.

2

The Two-Diagonal Sequence

Our #1 Clear vs 10-Man

Setup: Bring the high D-man and a middie 10 yards below the midline — between midline and restraining line. This forces the ride's geometry.

D1 → M3 (Diagonal 1) → A1 Up Far Sideline (Diagonal 2) · Goalie Must Recover
  1. Ball starts with D1 behind the goal — he's the QB.
  2. M1 and M2 cut hard — pulling their defenders with them.
  3. A3 (far-side attackman) cuts back to the ball — his man must stay onside, so he's momentarily free.
  4. D1 hits M3 coming through the middle at 45°.
  5. M3 throws the diagonal skip to A1 cutting up the far sideline.
  6. Ball is now at midline — their goalie MUST sprint back to the cage.
  7. You have a 6v5 going to goal. Don't settle. Push and score.
Key Rule

Never throw across the middle. That's where the double-team lives. Always diagonal or up the sideline. Short passes over long ones.

Why It Works
  • Diagonal passes beat zone pressure by attacking the seams between riders
  • The second we're at midline, their goalie is in no-man's-land
  • Their slide geometry is stretched to the limit — one breakdown = goal
Counter 03

Crease Flash

The moment their goalie commits, our attackmen flash to the empty crease. They become the outlet AND the finisher — goalie-on-attack is the weakest matchup on the field.

3

Attackman to Empty Crease

Hidden Release Valve

When the ride's goalie is covering your X-attackman, have him fade to the crease while your D-man with the ball looks him off. The goalie can't guard what he can't reach.

Why It Works
  • Goalie-on-attack is always a mismatch — even at D1 level
  • The crease is UNGUARDED — their scheme abandoned it
  • Your attackman has a 6x6 goal cage staring at him
  • Even a bad feed results in a cage shot
  1. A1 reads that the goalie is out of the crease
  2. A1 fakes like he's going to receive the ball outside to sell his man
  3. A1 flashes to the empty crease — stays on the far post
  4. D1 lofts a pass over the top — not too hard, arched over the riders
  5. A1 catches and immediately shoots — don't look to pass, shoot now
Cue

If the pass isn't there, A1 runs the ball down the field. He's now an offensive runner with a fresh stick and open space — free fast break.

Counter 04

Exploit the Onside Rule

Only 6 offensive players can legally be in the offensive half at once. When their goalie crosses midfield, someone has to stay back — which means we automatically have an advantage on the back half.

4

The Onside Trap Attack

Rules-Based Exploit

The second their goalie crosses the midline, their defense must abandon one of your attackmen to stay legal (can't have 7 players offensively). That attackman is now uncovered.

Why It Works
  • It's a rulebook exploit — the defense cannot stop it without penalty
  • Guaranteed 2v1 or 3v2 in our offensive half
  • One long outlet and it's a fast break
  1. One attackman stays behind the restraining line (ALWAYS) — he's our safety valve
  2. Two attackmen pressure-release up to the midline — pull defenders
  3. As goalie crosses midline, count bodies — their defense has to leave someone
  4. D1 throws the long outlet over the top to the held-back attackman
  5. Our attackman catches clean in his zone, turns and attacks the empty cage
Who Holds?

Our X-attackman (typically the best shooter) always holds the back. He's the trigger for this counter. Communicate: "I'M HOT" — means he's ready for the outlet.

Counter 05

Beat the 9.5 (Partial Ride)

Smart teams have stopped running full 10-mans. Notre Dame (2023 champs) runs a "9.5" where the goalie sits 10-15 yards out — not fully committed. Same counters apply, but execution must be faster.

5

The 9.5 / Partial Ride Counter

Modern Evolution

In the 9.5, their goalie hangs 10-15 yards off the goal line. He's not fully committed, which means:

  • The cage is less empty — full-field shots are tougher
  • Their goalie can re-engage faster — our 6v5 window is shorter (3-5 sec vs 8 sec)
  • They still commit a short-stick to cover your 4th attacker — mismatch alert
How to Beat It
  • Move the ball quicker — two passes max, not three
  • Attack the mismatch — our attackman vs their short-stick is a dodge opportunity
  • Pre-plan the first two passes — no hesitation
  • If the goalie hesitates to re-engage, shoot — Virginia's McCabe Millon scored off exactly this
The Tell

Watch the goalie's feet on the clear. If he's shuffling laterally, he's in 9.5 mode. If he's sprinting forward, it's full 10-man.

Same plays — faster tempo. Don't overthink it.

Install Plan

Practice Drills

Three drills to install every counter. Run them Tuesday/Thursday leading into any opponent that rides. 35 minutes of practice time.

Drill 1 — Ladder Clear vs 10-Man

20 MIN

Installs the two-diagonal clear in a controlled-to-live progression.

  1. Setup: 6 clearers (full D + goalie) vs 10 riders at the other end
  2. Phase 1 (5 min): Static 10-man. Ride is locked in position. Clearers walk through the two-diagonal — get the footwork right.
  3. Phase 2 (5 min): Slow-speed live. Ride can react but not sprint. Clearers must complete the clear in 3 passes.
  4. Phase 3 (10 min): FULL LIVE. 10-man ride goes 100%. Clearers must execute and attack to the goal on the other end.
  5. Scoring: +1 for a clean clear. +2 for a goal off the clear. -1 for a turnover.

Drill 2 — Shoot the Empty Net

10 MIN

Builds the confidence and skill for full-field empty-net shots. Poles and D-middies only.

  1. Setup: Goalie stands at midline (simulates the empty net scenario)
  2. Shooters line up at their own defensive restraining line (60-70 yards from the goal)
  3. Phase 1 (3 min): Stationary shots. Get the mechanics.
  4. Phase 2 (4 min): Shot on the run. Right and left hand.
  5. Phase 3 (3 min): Competition. Top 3 shooters earn the "green light" badge for games.

Drill 3 — Recognition Scrimmage

5 MIN

Builds the automatic "CAGE!" call and counter selection under game pressure.

  1. Setup: Full-field 10v10 scrimmage
  2. At random moments, coach calls "10-MAN" — the defense must immediately convert to a 10-man ride on the next possession
  3. Clearing team must: (a) yell "CAGE!" (b) identify the goalie's position (c) execute the correct counter
  4. After each rep, 15-second chalk talk — what did we see, what did we run, was it right?
  5. Rotate: every player should be on the clearing side at least twice
